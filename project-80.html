🟡 HOW TO APPROACH THE INTERVIEW

RULE 1: DO NOT start drawing immediately
→ Ask 3-5 clarifying questions first
→ Shows maturity — junior engineers jump to solutions
→ Senior engineers DEFINE the problem before solving it

RULE 2: Structure your whiteboard in LAYERS (not spaghetti)
→ Top: Sources (left to right)
→ Middle: Processing (left to right = flow)
→ Bottom: Serving (consumers)
→ Side: Cross-cutting concerns (security, monitoring, governance)

RULE 3: Name specific AWS services — not generic boxes
→ NOT: "some ETL tool" → YES: "AWS Glue with PySpark"
→ NOT: "a database" → YES: "Redshift Serverless on RA3"
→ Shows you KNOW the tools, not just the concepts

RULE 4: Every decision must have a WHY
→ NOT: "we'll use S3" → YES: "S3 because $0.023/GB, 11 nines durability,
   and it's the integration hub — Glue, Athena, Redshift Spectrum all read it natively"

RULE 5: Proactively mention trade-offs BEFORE asked
→ "I chose Redshift Serverless over Provisioned because at this scale,
    pay-per-query is cheaper — but if query volume exceeds 8 hours/day
    sustained, we'd switch to Provisioned RA3"
→ Shows DEPTH — you've considered alternatives

💻 MINUTE 18-33: DEFEND EVERY DECISION

Defense 1: "Why S3 as the center, not a database?"

INTERVIEWER: "Why not just use a big Redshift cluster as the 
              single source of truth? Why all this S3 complexity?"

YOUR ANSWER:

"Three reasons:

 1. COST: 2.8TB in S3 = $64/month. 2.8TB in Redshift = $700/month (RA3).
    S3 is 10x cheaper for storage. Data grows every day — that gap widens.

 2. DECOUPLING: S3 is the universal connector.
    Glue reads it, Athena queries it, Redshift COPYs from it,
    Spectrum queries it in-place, SageMaker reads it for training.
    If I stored everything in Redshift, every consumer would need
    a Redshift connection — creating a bottleneck.

 3. REPROCESSABILITY: If a Glue job has a bug and writes wrong data
    to Silver, I still have the original raw data in Bronze.
    I fix the job, reprocess from Bronze, and Silver is correct.
    If Redshift was my only store and I wrote bad data, it's gone.

 The trade-off: more components, more orchestration complexity.
 But that complexity is manageable with Step Functions and 
 EventBridge — and it gives us a platform that scales to 50TB
 without re-architecture."

REFERENCES: Projects 1 (S3 internals), 44 (Medallion), 38 (Spectrum)

Defense 2: "Why Glue over EMR/Spark on EC2?"

INTERVIEWER: "Glue is expensive per DPU-hour. Why not just run 
              Spark on EMR or EC2 for half the cost?"

YOUR ANSWER:

"You're right that Glue DPU cost ($0.44/hr) is higher than 
 EMR spot instances (~$0.10/hr). But total cost of ownership 
 is different from compute cost:

 1. OPERATIONAL OVERHEAD: EMR requires cluster management — 
    scaling, patching, Spark version upgrades, YARN tuning.
    With 0 data engineers today and 200 tables to migrate,
    we need the team focused on PIPELINES, not infrastructure.
    Glue is serverless — zero cluster management.

 2. GLUE ECOSYSTEM: Data Catalog, Crawlers, Job Bookmarks,
    Schema Registry, Data Quality — all integrated.
    On EMR, I'd need to build or integrate each of these separately.
    That's 2-3 months of engineering time saved.

 3. COST AT OUR SCALE: Our largest job processes 120GB.
    At 20 DPU × 30 minutes = $4.40 per run.
    That's $132/month for daily runs.
    EMR equivalent: ~$50/month compute + $80/month engineering time
    for maintenance = roughly the same.

 THE CROSSOVER POINT: At ~100 DPU sustained for 8+ hours daily,
 EMR becomes significantly cheaper. We're nowhere near that.
 If we hit that scale, we migrate specific jobs to EMR — 
 Glue and EMR can coexist reading the same S3 data.

 I'd revisit this decision at the 1-year mark."

REFERENCES: Projects 25 (Glue ETL), 33 (Bookmarks), 69 (cost optimization)

Defense 3: "Why Redshift Serverless over Provisioned?"

INTERVIEWER: "Redshift Serverless is new and expensive per RPU. 
              Why not proven provisioned nodes?"

YOUR ANSWER:

"For MegaRetail's current workload — 20 analysts, batch dashboards,
 moderate concurrency — Serverless is the right choice:

 1. UTILIZATION PATTERN: Analysts query 8AM-6PM weekdays.
    Dashboards refresh at 6AM. That's ~12 hours of active use.
    Provisioned: pay 24/7 = $0.25/hr × 2 nodes × 24hr × 30 = $360/month.
    Serverless: pay only when querying = 32 RPU × $0.375 × 12hr × 22 days = $3,168.

    Wait — that's MORE expensive. Let me recalculate:
    
    Actually, Serverless auto-scales to ZERO when idle.
    Provisioned with pause: $0.25/hr × 2 dc2.large × 12hr × 30 = $180/month.
    
    Hmm, at this small scale, provisioned with pause schedules 
    might actually be cheaper. Good catch.

    HERE'S WHY I STILL CHOOSE SERVERLESS:
    → No capacity planning — RPU scales automatically
    → No pause/resume management (queries fail during resume)
    → Concurrency scaling is automatic (no WLM tuning)
    → New team with 0 Redshift experience — less to manage

    I'd switch to Provisioned RA3 if sustained usage exceeds
    8 hours/day consistently for 3+ months.

 2. MIGRATION SPEED: Serverless is ready in minutes.
    Provisioned requires sizing decisions upfront.
    During migration, I want to iterate fast — resize later.

 This is a 'start Serverless, graduate to Provisioned' strategy."

REFERENCES: Projects 67 (Serverless), 39 (optimization), 40 (WLM)

NOTE: ADMITTING THE COST COMPARISON ISN'T CLEAR-CUT shows HONESTY.

Defense 4: "Why 4 accounts instead of 1?"

INTERVIEWER: "4 AWS accounts seems like overkill. Why not just 
              use IAM policies in a single account?"

YOUR ANSWER:

"Three reasons, in order of importance:

 1. BLAST RADIUS: If someone misconfigures an IAM policy in the 
    ingestion account, it cannot affect Redshift in the serving account.
    In a single account, one bad policy could expose everything.
    SOX compliance requires this separation for financial data.

 2. BILLING ISOLATION: We can see exactly what ingestion costs
    vs processing vs serving. In one account, cost attribution
    requires complex tagging discipline that teams always forget.

 3. SERVICE LIMITS: S3 request limits, Glue concurrent jobs,
    Redshift connections — all per-account. Separate accounts
    prevent one workload from starving another.

 THE TRADE-OFF: Cross-account IAM is more complex.
 Every S3 access needs bucket policies + AssumeRole.
 Every Glue catalog reference needs resource policies.
 
 But this complexity is CONFIGURATION, not CODE.
 Set it up once with CloudFormation, and it's automated forever.
 The security benefit far outweighs the setup cost.

 If budget or team size made 4 accounts impractical,
 I'd consolidate to 2: one for data (ingestion + processing)
 and one for serving + governance. Never go below 2."

REFERENCES: Projects 56-61 (cross-account), 74 (Lake Formation)

Defense 5: "What about the MongoDB clickstream?"

INTERVIEWER: "You said Kinesis Firehose for MongoDB. Why not DMS?"

YOUR ANSWER:

"DMS supports MongoDB as a source, so it COULD work.
 But there are three reasons I prefer Kinesis here:

 1. LATENCY: Supply chain needs 15-minute freshness.
    DMS CDC for MongoDB uses polling (not change streams natively 
    in all versions). Kinesis Firehose with MongoDB Change Streams
    gives true real-time — events arrive within seconds.

 2. FAN-OUT: Clickstream data feeds multiple consumers —
    S3 for the lake, potentially real-time dashboards later,
    maybe a recommendation engine. Kinesis Data Streams allows
    multiple consumers. DMS writes to one target only.

 3. VOLUME: 300GB of clickstream with potentially thousands of
    events per second during peak traffic. Kinesis handles 
    this natively with shard scaling. DMS replication instance 
    would need significant sizing for this throughput.

 THE TRADE-OFF: Kinesis requires custom code for the producer
 (MongoDB Change Stream → Kinesis). DMS is configuration-only.
 But the latency and fan-out benefits justify the code investment.

 For PostgreSQL and Oracle — DMS is the right choice because 
 those are traditional RDBMS with well-supported CDC via binlog/redo log,
 and we don't need sub-minute latency for those sources."

REFERENCES: Projects 72 (Kinesis), 31 (Glue JDBC), 42 (CDC)

Defense 6: "How do you handle the CSV vendors?"

INTERVIEWER: "12 vendors dropping CSVs — what could go wrong?"

YOUR ANSWER:

"Everything. Vendor data is the most unreliable source. My strategy:

 1. LANDING ZONE: Dedicated S3 bucket with per-vendor prefixes.
    Each vendor gets their own IAM user with PutObject-only permission
    to their specific prefix. They cannot read other vendors' data.

 2. SCHEMA VALIDATION: S3 event triggers Lambda that validates:
    → File name matches expected pattern
    → Column count matches schema registry
    → No unexpected NULL in required columns
    → File size within expected range (detect truncated uploads)
    If validation fails → file moved to quarantine/ prefix → SNS alert.

 3. IDEMPOTENCY: Vendors sometimes re-upload the same file.
    We track processed files in DynamoDB.
    If file hash matches a previously processed file → skip it.

 4. LATE ARRIVALS: Vendor misses a day? 
    Data freshness alarm detects it within 2 hours.
    Automated email to vendor + internal team.

 5. SCHEMA CHANGES: Vendor adds a column without telling us?
    Glue Crawler detects schema evolution.
    ResolveChoice in Glue ETL handles it gracefully.
    Alert sent: 'schema changed for vendor X — review needed.'

 This is actually where most production incidents originate.
 I'd invest more monitoring here than anywhere else."

REFERENCES: Projects 17-18 (S3 events), 45 (schema evolution), 
            50 (DLQ), 68 (data quality)

Defense 7: "How do you handle EU data residency?"

INTERVIEWER: "Walk me through the EU requirement in more detail."

YOUR ANSWER:

"The approach has three parts:

 1. CLASSIFICATION: Lake Formation tags mark every column.
    → pii=true/false
    → region=EU/US/GLOBAL
    → sensitivity=high/medium/low
    Tags are inherited: if a table is tagged region=EU,
    all columns inherit that tag.

 2. DATA ROUTING: During ingestion, DMS routes EU customer records
    to S3 buckets in eu-west-1. The routing logic is based on
    a country/region column in the source data.
    Non-PII data (aggregated revenue, product stats) goes to us-east-2.

 3. PROCESSING: Separate Glue ETL jobs run in eu-west-1 for EU data.
    Same code, different deployment region.
    Gold layer aggregates (no PII) are replicated to us-east-2
    using S3 Cross-Region Replication with a filter rule
    that only replicates objects tagged 'pii=false'.

 4. SERVING: EU analysts query eu-west-1 Redshift/Athena.
    US analysts see global aggregates in us-east-2.
    Lake Formation prevents any cross-region PII leakage.

 THE TRADE-OFF: Running parallel pipelines in 2 regions roughly
 doubles processing cost. But GDPR fines are up to 4% of global
 revenue — the cost of compliance is trivial by comparison.

 For APAC (future): same pattern, deployed to ap-southeast-1."

REFERENCES: Projects 47 (S3 CRR), 74 (Lake Formation tags), 75 (multi-region DR)

Defense 8: "What if a Glue job corrupts Gold layer data?"

INTERVIEWER: "Your Gold table has wrong numbers. CEO sees bad dashboard.
              What happens?"

YOUR ANSWER:

"This exact scenario happened in my design — let me walk through
 the DETECTION → DIAGNOSIS → RECOVERY chain:

 DETECTION (< 2 minutes):
 → Data freshness checker runs every 15 minutes
 → Data quality rules check business invariants:
   'daily revenue must be within 3 standard deviations of 30-day average'
 → If revenue drops 50%, quality rule fires → CloudWatch alarm
 → Composite alarm triggers Step Functions recovery

 DIAGNOSIS (< 5 minutes):
 → Alert includes lineage: 'gold.finance_summary.net_revenue traces to 
   silver.orders.order_total → bronze.postgres.orders.amount'
 → On-call runs: lineage_cli.py investigate --node gold.finance_summary.net_revenue
 → Lineage shows: the Glue job at Silver layer had a new filter
   that dropped valid rows (schema evolution wasn't handled)

 RECOVERY (< 10 minutes):
 → Bronze data is IMMUTABLE — it's correct
 → Fix the Glue job (remove bad filter)
 → Reprocess: Bronze → Silver → Gold
 → Glue Job Bookmarks reset to reprocess affected date range
 → Gold table refreshed → Redshift COPY reloads → Dashboard correct

 PREVENTION:
 → Data quality gate between Silver and Gold (Project 68)
 → If quality score < 99.5%, Gold is NOT updated
 → Dashboard shows STALE but CORRECT data instead of FRESH but WRONG data
 → This is a critical design principle: better stale than wrong.

 TOTAL TIME TO RECOVERY: 10-15 minutes (vs hours without this system)"

REFERENCES: Projects 68 (quality), 77 (lineage), 78 (SLA), 44 (medallion)
